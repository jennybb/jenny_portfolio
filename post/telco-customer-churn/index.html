Telco Customer Churn

December 6, 2020

Customer churn has been an important topic in the telecom industry because it directly affects company’s profitability. Churn describes that customers stop using a company’s product or service during a certain time frame. Considering that the cost of retaining an existing customer is much lower than the cost of acquiring a new customer, telecom companies often provide competitive offers to customers who tend to churn. The object of this project is to help telecom companies predict whether a customer will churn or will not churn.

In this report, will mainly discuss three machine learning algorithms: Random Forest, Logistic Regression, Gradient Boosting Machine (GBM). For the full analysis and code, you can visit my GitHub repository here: https://github.com/jennybb/Telecom-Churn-Prediction. And I also made an interactive web application: https://jc-project-1.herokuapp.com/https://telcom-customer-churn-project.herokuapp.com/

Abstract:

This project aims to predict whether a telecom customer will churn so that telecom companies can take actions to retrain their customers. We are using the telecom customer churn data originally provided by the IBM Watson Analytics and available in Kaggle. We applied three types of mining algorithms, classification, regression, and clustering to this binary classification problem. The specific models we used are random forest, gradient boosting machine, logistic regression and K-Nearest Neighbors.

Dataset:

Downloaded from Kaggle, the telecom customer churn dataset contains 7043 rows (customers) and 21 columns (features). Each row represents a customer and includes information about customers who left within the last month, services that each customer has signed up for such as phone, multiple lines, internet, online security, online backup, device protection, tech support, and streaming TV and movies. It also includes demographic info about customers such as gender, age range, and if they have partners and dependents. The target column is “Churn” which has binary values, ‘Yes’ or ‘No’.

This plot shows us the correlation between our target and other features. We can see that the “Contract Month to Month”, “Online Security_No” and” Tech Support_No” have strong positive correlations with our target column. It means a consumer having a month-to-month contract with the company, not signing up for online security or not signing up for tech support, are more likely to leave. However, the correlation plot also tells us if a Consumer has a tenure, they are less likely to leave the company. Because there is a strong negative correlation between the tenure feature and the target column. The correlation plot also reminds us, if the company wants to have more customers stay, they should pay more attention to those who have month-to-month contracts, not signing up for online security or not signing up for tech support. For example, the company can offer some discounts or reimbursement.

Machine Learning Methods

1. Random Forest

Random forest is an ensemble of decision tree algorithms. It is an extension of bootstrap aggregation (bagging) of decision trees and can be used for regression and classification problems. Random forest tries to remove correlation by randomly sampling training data columns at each split point. Therefore, each tree uses different feature subsets for the prediction.

Here we selected six parameters for model tuning: n_estimators=10, max_depth=7, max_features=’auto’, min_samples_split=5, min_samples_leaf=2, and bootstrap=False.

Tenure, total charges and monthly charges are necessary predictors. Tenure means a customer is tenure for how long. This is obvious to see, when a customer is tenure for a long time may mean he would like to spend more time on this platform, which is the same as the charges. A customer is more willing to pay for the platform, he will be more willing to stay. Also, monthly charges and total charges are two related features, it’s not surprising to see they are both in the top predictors list. For other top predictors, we can see, customers who have no technical support, no online security and who prefer using checks for payment are more likely to stay.

2. Gradient Boosting Machine (GBM)

Gradient Boosting is an ensemble machine learning technique that can also be used for classification. The ensemble approach relies on combining a large number of relatively weak simple models to obtain a stronger ensemble prediction. In gradient boosting machines (GBM), the learning procedure consecutively fits new models to provide a more accurate estimate of the response variable.

In total, we have tuned five parameters. The best parameters set we have are “learning_rate = 0.08”, “loss = deviance”, “max_depth = 2”, “n_eistimator = 110” and “subsample = 0.5”.

The three most important features obtained from the GBM are “Contract month to month”, “tenure”, and “Internet Service Fiber Optic”. Compared to the feature importance obtained from the Random Forest model, “tenure” is still among the top 3. The other two features are more important in the GBM than in RF. For users who have month to month contracts, they have better mobility than those who have a fixed term contract with telecom companies. For this group of users, they don’t have to worry about the consequences of breaking a contract when they make decisions to leave companies.

3. Logistic Regression

Logistic Regression is a Machine Learning algorithm which is used for classification problems, it is a predictive analysis algorithm and based on the concept of probability. Similar to the linear SVM model, there are also two important hyper parameters in the Logistic Regression model, which are ‘C’ and ‘penalty’. During the hyper parameter tuning, the best result we got is C=6.15, penalty is l2.


Here we will be focus on the abosulate value of features' weight. The result here is quite similar to Random Forest’s result. It seems like “Tenure”, “Monthly Charges” and “Total Charges” feautres are the top 3 important predictors.

Conclusion

We generated a model performance master table that includes all the algorithms we applied to our data. We used five consistent model performance evaluation methods (i.e. use 10 fold cross validation to get the prediction accuracy). The table is sorted in the decreasing order of the model performance for the ROC_AUC Score metric. For model comparison, we decided to use AUC Score as the key primary metric. The reason is that AUC measures how well a parameter can distinguish between two diagnostic groups, which is the same as our goal: we want to ensure that the two target labels are separable.

Using F1-scores as the key evaluation metrics, the rankings of our machine learning models are: Logistic Regression (59.94%), Gradient Boosting Machine (59.40%), Linear SVM (58.40%), Random Forest (55.87%), and K-Nearest Neighbor (47.76%).
